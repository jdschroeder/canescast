The Young Guns: How do they stack up?

February 23, 2012 - by Josh Schroeder

It’s no secret that the Lethbridge Hurricanes, beginning to emerge from a rebuild, have relied a great deal on the performance of rookies this season. Nine first year players have been regulars in the line-up for the Hurricanes this season, and we’ve seen some good performances from that cohort. Since these players are going to become the core of the team in the next couple of seasons, I thought it would be interesting to look at the impact of Hurricanes’ rookies vis-à-vis vis those of other WHL teams this season.

I decided to focus on offensive production, since goals scored is a simple indicator that is easy to track. First I took a look at each team’s total goals scored next to the number of goals scored by rookies.

You’ll notice that there’s a pretty good correlation here between how many goals a team has scored and where they are located in the standings, and the rookie contribution appears pretty much random.

We could compare the number of goals scored by rookies for each team, but that doesn’t take into consideration how big a contribution that total is. Instead, let’s see what it looks like as a percentage of total goals score by the team.

We are looking at two sets of data, one takes all rookies into account, and one that omits import players. The reason for this is to reduce the effect of older “rookies” who are sometimes already NHL draft picks before joining a WHL team. Several of these players seem more like exceptional cases that tend to skew the data Victor Rask in Calgary being a prime example. The effect of removing one or two rookie imports is pretty evident when you look at Calgary (Rask and Gogolev), Vancouver (Tvrdon), and Kamloops (Bozon). This doesn’t take into consideration other “mature” rookies such as the case of Mitch Maxwell as a 19 year old a few seasons ago.

As I expected, the Hurricanes are on the upper end of goal percentage contributed by rookies, regardless or whether imports are included or not. There are a few ways to interpret this:

  1. The Hurricanes’ veteran goal scoring has been sub-standard this year, meaining that an average amount of goals by rookies constitutes a greater share of the total.

  2. Thanks to injuries, the freshmen have been given more ice time and more opportunity, and have naturally seen their production higher than we would otherwise expect.

  3. The Hurricanes have a strong contingent of young players that are providing a substantial share of the team’s offensive production.

I think you can make a case that all three of these interpretations play into the Hurricanes’ situation to some extent. Had Austin Fyten and Michael Sofillas been healthy this season, perhaps there are two fewer rookies regularly in the line-up, and perhaps the offensive output of veterans is greater.

Given that both the percentage share of goals and the sum total of goals provided by rookies are among the highest in the league, I think it’s safe to say that regardless of other circumstances, the Hurricanes do have a strong crop of rookies. Should the young core of the team continue to develop and stay healthy, optimism toward coming seasons appears to be well placed.
